Nashville City Paper

Jackson's Colorful Goodbye
By Drew Walen

12/12/02


Parting can be such sweet sorrow. Sometimes it can be caffeinated too,
especially when you say goodbye from one of Nashville’s favorite coffee shops.
Amy Jackson, a local artist who only recently came onto the scene, is leaving
Nashville for D.C., but is holding one final showing of her brilliant photography
work at Fido in Hillsboro Village.

A self-proclaimed poet who played with photography and painting a good part of
her life, Jackson got serious about her art work in ’93. "I had been experiencing a
lot of chronic pain due to a jaw dysfunction (TMJ)," she said. "I started to paint
because I needed to express myself. I couldn’t make facial expressions or talk. I
had to keep my stress level really low because it’s a stress-related condition.
Painting helped."

Along with painting, Jackson delved deeper into her photography. "I’ve taken a lot
of bad rolls of film," she admitted. "[My training] has been completely trial and
error. I would take pictures of things I like. If I find something fascinating, I would
get really close to it. After ’93, I started using photography as a way to capture
things that looked like paintings. I think they started to influence each other."
In 1995, Jackson moved to Nashville and continued to paint and dabble in
photography, though it took her until 2000 to begin to feel confident enough to
approach local galleries for showings. ruby green gave her a chance with a series
of images taken at the Chilton’s Industrial Machine Shop featuring subject matter
like rusted-out industrial machinery and metal plates, 15-foot drills with cryptic
writing on them, and piled up drill bits and metal shavings. The items could never
be identified at the angles and depth of field taken, thus creating enchanting,
nearly abstract pieces.

"I look for beauty in odd places and in things that other people think are junk. I
love the randomness in the Chilton series," Jackson commented.

Another collection of images, which more closely reflects her background in
painting, includes a series of broken glass pictures. Some are natural and others
digitally enhanced, producing the visual essence of a watercolor painting. "Mostly
I’m just playing with the light, the brightness and the contrast. It brings out all this
random stuff you wouldn’t think was there."

Jackson’s farewell show at Fido will feature never-before-seen images from these
two collections. She hopes to continue developing her connections to local
galleries for future exhibitions in the coming years.

Amy Jackson’s final show in Nashville takes place Monday, Dec. 16, through
Jan. 16 at Fido, 1812 21st Ave. S. An opening reception takes place 6-8 p.m.
Monday. For more information call 777-3436 or visit www.amyjackson.com.
